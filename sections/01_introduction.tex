  Introduction

  Hints
  -----
  - Paragraph 1: establish the field context with 2–3 high-impact citations.
    Show why the area is active and why it matters.
  - Paragraph 2: describe the opportunity or problem that creates a gap.
    Make the reader feel the tension.
  - Paragraph 3: state the research question. Make it precise and
    literature-searchable. Bold it or use \textit{}.
  - Enumerated list: situate your question at the intersection of N fields.
    Each item should name its section reference so the reader can navigate.
  - Final paragraph before subsection: briefly characterise existing work and
    why it falls short (one sentence per prior body of work).

  Citation style examples
  -----------------------
  \citet{Key2024}       → Author (2024) — use when the author is the subject.
                          e.g., "\citet{Houlsby2019} introduced bottleneck adapters"
  \citep{Key2024}       → (Author, 2024) — use for parenthetical citations.
                          e.g., "adapters have been widely adopted \citep{Hu2022}."
  \citet{A2020,B2021}   → Author (2020); Author (2021) — multiple in-text.
  \citep{A2020,B2021}   → (Author, 2020; Author, 2021) — multiple parenthetical.

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

 Context paragraph
 Introduce the field. 2–3 sentences establishing dominance/relevance.
 Use \citep{} for supporting references.
REPLACE: Introduce the research area and its importance.
Cite foundational works \citep{ExampleFoundation2020, ExampleSurvey2022}.

 Opportunity / tension paragraph
 Describe the structural property or observation that enables your research
 direction, or the limitation that motivates it.
REPLACE: Describe the key property, opportunity, or limitation that motivates
this review. Make the gap visible without stating it explicitly yet.

 Research question paragraph
 State the RQ in bold or italics. Be precise. Then link to the methodology
 section where it is operationalised.
\textbf{The research question} addressed by this review is: \textit{REPLACE:
state your main research question here.} More specifically: REPLACE: state the
operationalised form of the question — what would a positive or negative answer
look like in the literature?
Section~\ref{sec:methodology} operationalises this question into a
literature-searchable form.

 Intersection paragraph — enumerate the contributing fields
This problem sits at the intersection of N active research fields:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Field 1 name}
    (Section~\ref{sec:lit_cluster_1}, Section~\ref{sec:lit_cluster_2}):
    One sentence on the relevant question this field addresses.
  \item \textbf{Field 2 name}
    (Section~\ref{sec:lit_cluster_3}, Section~\ref{sec:lit_cluster_4}):
    One sentence on the relevant question this field addresses.
  \item \textbf{Field 3 name}
    (Section~\ref{sec:lit_cluster_5}):
    One sentence on the relevant question this field addresses.
\end{enumerate}

 Prior work paragraph
 One sentence per body of prior work, naming the key systems/papers and
 stating precisely what each one does NOT do. End by noting the gap.
REPLACE: body of work A \citep{ExampleA2023} does X but not Y.
Body of work B \citep{ExampleB2022} does Y but not Z.
Each addresses part of the problem, but their intersection has not been
addressed as an integrated whole.

\subsection{Review Structure}
\label{sec:introduction:structure}

 Signposting paragraph
 One sentence per section, in order. Use \ref{} consistently.
Section~\ref{sec:background} provides the technical background.
Section~\ref{sec:methodology} describes the SLR methodology.
Sections~\ref{sec:lit_cluster_1}--\ref{sec:lit_cluster_5} review the
five bodies of related work. Section~\ref{sec:synthesis_gap} synthesises
the literature into a gap analysis. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.
